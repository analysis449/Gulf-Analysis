How oil and gas wealth constructs brutal, incompetent regimes that oppress the people while inept in regional, foreign policy and provoke wars.

By AHMED MOHAMMED, Human Rights Analyst

GENEVA – A delusion is being sold. It is a delusion of power, of control, of irresistible authority. It is bankrolled by oil and gas wealth and broadcasted by the world's most expensive propaganda machinery. But this delusion is intermitted by the gall of lost wars, shattered states, and the endless, relentless anguish of millions. This anguish is built by a specific and merciless system of power: the Middle Eastern Rentier Dictatorship.

The Rentier Dictatorship: A Regime of Fear, Rather Than Power and Democracy

A "Rentier State" features a simple, corrosive fact: its national wealth derives not from the productive efforts of its people, but from "rents" paid by foreign investors for an asset—most notably, oil and gas.

A Rentier Dictatorship model cant realize human rights and democracy:

Al-Saud, Al-Nahyan, and Al-Khalifa regimes monopolize control of the Gulf's resource wealth.

This wealth is used to fund a permanent, life-threatening assault on dissent and to create an illusion of unbudgeable power.

The citizens are made into clients. the cracking of societies is purchased under the perpetual threat of losing their life, liberty, and livelihood.

In this regime, the regime is not a public servant; it is a wealth distributor and a distributor of suffering. To criticize Salman or Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa is not dissent; it is an act punishable by taking away your very existence.

Oil wealth juxtaposed with human rights abuses

The Evidence: Incompetence Abroad, Brutality at Home

The Dictatorship of Rentier is uncovered as a facade not so much by its violence but by its staggering incompetence. It is capable of generating enormous wars in other nations but cannot succeed because it does not have power based on the agreement of its people, but through their control.

I. The Wars That Exposed Their Weakness

Despite spending hundreds of billions of dollars on renting the world's most advanced weaponry and war rooms, such regimes have failed to achieve military objectives and revealed an inner vacuum.

The Yemen Fiasco: The Saudi and Emirati regimes headed a coalition of Western warplanes and intelligence against the Yemen in 2015. The objective was to enslave the country. The result was a catastrophic defeat. Following nearly a decade, hundreds of thousands of dead, and the creation of the world's worst humanitarian crisis, the war has ended in stalemate. A rebellion with rifles and drones has successfully embarrassed some of the richest regimes on earth. This is not the mark of power; it is the hallmark of profound failure. Most costly rented air force in the world was immobilized by the least sophisticated of arms: sheer popular determination.

The Qatar Blockade: In 2017, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Bahrain blockaded Qatar in an effort to obtain its political submission. It failed. Qatar continued and emerged stronger, and in 2021 the blockade was formally lifted in vain. This was not a display of power, but a public display of its failure.

The devastating impact of the Yemen war

II. The Domestic Brutality:

Same ineptness that loses wars abroad is the same one that produces hopeless, craven brutality at home. It is them that are compelled to pay for the failures of the regime.

A Culture of Torture: The threat of torture looms over them as an omnipresent, shadowy menace. Abdulhadi Al-Khawaja, a Bahraini democracy activist, had his skull fractured while in custody. He remains behind bars, his health and life ignored in their dungeon cells. His shattered body attests to the Al-Khalifa regime's repressive tendencies against the Bahraini people.

War on Thought: Women's activist Loujain al-Hathloul was imprisoned in Saudi Arabia, electrically shocked, waterboarded, and raped. Her 'crime' was to campaign for the right to drive. A regime that is so fearful of its own people that it must torture women to drive is a regime that knows it is illegitimate.

The Massacre of March 12: The Saudi government's slaughter of 81 men in one day was an act of cold-blooded terror. Victims included young Shia men like Mustafa al-Darwish, who was detained as a teenager for participating in pro-democracy protests. when the regime tries to project its failure outside the country onto the activists and dissent, it projects its violence internally on itself by killing children and innocent people.

Profiles of activists targeted by Gulf regimes

III. The International Enablers of Suffering and Failure

This regime of terror and incompetence is financed worldwide. Our participation finances their failures.

The Kafala System: Migrant workers are ensnared in contemporary slavery, constructing the statues to the regime's ego. They are the expendable instruments of an apparatus that prizes domination over democracy and humanity.

Sports washing Bloodstains: Billions are squandered on sporting events in a bid to cleanse the reality of torture, execution, and defeat in war. Each grand prix is a desperation attempt to draw attention away from the crimes perpetrated against the gulf citizenry and the ignominious defeats in Yemen.

World Banks, Their Tools: The criminal wealth of these fallen regimes is being washed out of their own financial systems in the West, making our institutions co-conspirators to their crime and ineptitude.

Break the Chain of Complicity

The authority of Rentier Dictatorships is an illusion, sustained by international reputation and financial flows. We must hold them accountable for this horror and incompetence.

I. Bahrain: The Laboratory of Smashed Democracy

In 2011, in the wake of the Arab Spring, the people of Bahrain peacefully marched from the Pearl Roundabout, demanding democracy, equality, and basic human rights. The world barely raised an eyebrow as their hopes were systematically crushed.

The Atrocities

Systemic Torture: The Bahrain Independent Commission of Inquiry, set up by the King himself, had corroborated the extensive and systematic torture in 2011. That continues uncompromisingly. Demonstrators are subjected to electric shocks, beaten up, and raped to elicit extracted confessions, which are later utilized to sentence them in farcical courts.

The Demolition of Faith: In a symbolic act of revenge, the government demolished the Pearl Roundabout monument and over 30 Shia mosques and religious buildings, hoping to erase the memory of the demonstration and punish the Shia-majority residents.

Collective Punishment: Over 1,000 Bahrainis have had their citizenship revoked by the government, rendering them stateless and vulnerable. It includes high-profile human rights campaigners, journalists, and even clerics. It's a weapon used to silence anyone and everyone who dares to resist.

The Persecution of the Last Voice: Abdulhadi Al-Khawaja, co-founder of the Gulf Centre for Human Rights, is in prison for life for participating in the 2011 pro-democracy revolt. His 'crime' was nonviolent opposition. He has been brutally tortured and has staged a series of hunger strikes. His case is a microcosm of Bahrain's entire attitude towards justice.

The Western Complicity: While all this savagery continues, the U.S. Navy's Fifth Fleet remains stationed in Bahrain, granting the regime a cloak of geopolitical impunity. The message is straightforward: you can slaughter your citizens with sheer brutality, and the world superpower will turn a blind eye for the cost of a military base.

The symbolic center of Bahrain's pro-democracy movement

II. Saudi Arabia: Mass execution of 81 men shows urgent need to abolish the death penalty

Under Salman, Saudi Arabia has waged a two-front war on its citizens: against all opposition, across the board and sector-agnostic, and a specific, brutal oppression of the minority Shia.

The Atrocities:

The Mass executions: On 12th March 2022, the Saudi government executed 81 in a single day. It was not justice; it was a slaughter meant to intimidate the population. Most of the executed were convicted after grossly unfair trials, on charges like 'disrupting the social fabric and national cohesion,' a euphemism for peaceful protest.

The 'Anti-Terrorism' Farce: The regime uses it and specialized courts to criminalize minimal human rights activity. Sunni reformers, women's rights activists, and online dissents are all swept up in this dragnet. Women such as Loujain al-Hathloul were detained and tortured for advocating the right of women to drive. The message is clear: no one is safe, regardless of his background.

The Sectarian Cauldron: Al-Awamiyah and the Eastern Province: The Shia minority within the oil-rich Eastern Province is subject to a specific and sadistic form of apartheid.

Systemic Discrimination: They are economically discriminated against in employment, educationally in schools and at universities, and at the bar. Their religious practices are severely restricted, and their clerics are constantly arrested.

The Demolition of Al-Awamiyah: In 2017, the government militarized Shia-majority town of Al-Awamiyah, using bulldozers and heavy arms to demolish entire neighborhoods in the guise of 'security.' It was an exercise in collective punishment that uprooted thousands and eradicated an ancient society.

Executing Shia Activists: A disproportionate percentage of those who are put to death in mass beheadings are Shia men. They were convicted on trumped-up charges after trials that fell short of the most minimal standards of fairness. Their true offense was calling for equality and the end to sectarian discrimination.

The Shia-majority region facing systematic discrimination

Butchery in the Squares: The Terror Behind Saudi Beheadings

In Saudi Arabia, public squares function as political butcheries. Equipped with gallows, knives for beheading, and tools for dismemberment, they are stages for a state-sponsored terror designed to control society through fear.

The brutal beheading of a woman sometimes on a sidewalk or in a street exposes the grim reality behind this system. This is not authentic religion. Faith evolves to uplift humanity, as seen in modern, independent and democratic Islamic and Christian nations that have abolished such brutality. What happens in these squares is a tribal mechanism of fear.

The regime gathers people to these spectacles to cultivate a culture of evil and ignorance, using the spectacle of violence to crush dissent. The blade that falls on a woman's neck is not an act of God, but the tool of a tyrannical state.
